Buying Guide

What to Look For in a Gutter Downspout

Getting a downspout extension right the first time comes down to a few measurements and material checks.

Measure your downspout diameter before buyingMost residential downspouts are 2x3 inch rectangular or round in 3-4 inch diameters — confirm your exact size to avoid a loose or overly tight fit that leaks at the connection.
Plan your routing path around landscapingA flexible extension lets you route water around walkways, flower beds, or AC units rather than running in a straight line, so map your yard before deciding on length.
Check for a way to secure the end positionExtensions that shift or get kicked out of place lose their effectiveness — look for stakes or splash blocks that anchor the discharge end in position.

Will this extension damage my lawn if left in place all season?
Flexible extensions generally sit lightly on top of grass and can be moved for mowing, though leaving one in a fixed spot for months may leave a slight impression that regrows quickly.
Does it fit standard downspouts?
It's designed for standard 4-inch diameter downspouts, which covers most common residential gutter systems, though it's worth measuring your own downspout before ordering.
Can it handle heavy rainfall without collapsing?
The flexible corrugated design generally handles typical heavy rainfall well, though buildup of leaves or debris inside can cause kinking if it isn't cleared periodically.
How far from the foundation should water be directed?
Most experts recommend directing water at least 5-10 feet away from the foundation, which this 16.5ft extension comfortably accommodates for most home layouts.
How does a flexible extension compare to a rigid downspout extension?
Rigid extensions resist kinking better but can't route around obstacles, while this flexible design trades some structural rigidity for the ability to navigate around landscaping features.
